## Google's Mission & "Googleyness" Principles

---

### The Mission: Organize the World's Information

Google's mission statement is "to organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful," and its vision is "to provide access to the world's information in one click."

Former Google People Chief Laszlo Bock once noted that the Google mission statement differed from many Fortune 500 companies in that it is **a moral goal, not a business goal.** The mission has remained essentially unchanged since the company's founding in 1998 — a rare feat for a company that grew into one of the largest on earth.

Its unofficial motto used to be "Don't be evil," which appeared in its employee Code of Conduct. That phrase was dropped in 2015 when Alphabet was formed, replaced with the broader directive: "Do the right thing — follow the law, act honorably, and treat each other with respect."

#### Google's Ten Things We Know to Be True

Google encodes its philosophy into ten principles it calls "things we know to be true," which it recommends as the foundation for understanding what Googleyness means:

1. Focus on the user and all else will follow
2. It's best to do one thing really, really well
3. Fast is better than slow
4. Democracy on the web works
5. You don't need to be at your desk to need an answer
6. You can make money without doing evil
7. There's always more information out there
8. The need for information crosses all borders
9. You can be serious without a suit
10. Great just isn't good enough

---

### What Is "Googleyness"?

From the earliest days, Googlers referred to actions as "Googley" or "not Googley." The word was never explicitly defined — everyone just sort of took it to mean "don't be evil," "do the right thing," or "be good to each other." Over time, it also became an informal test for cultural fit in interviews and performance reviews. Google eventually realized the term was being overloaded with meaning and, worse, risked becoming a source of unconscious bias — if "Googley" means something different to every employee, it could start meaning "is just like me."

Google has since reframed the concept from "culture fit" (will you fit in?) to **"culture add"** — will this candidate bring attitudes and character traits that are a *positive addition* to Google's working culture?

#### Laszlo Bock's Definitive Five Traits

Former Google Head of People Operations Laszlo Bock defined Googleyness in his 2015 book *Work Rules!* as five core attributes: **enjoying fun**, **intellectual humility** ("it's hard to learn if you can't admit that you might be wrong"), **conscientiousness** ("we want owners, not employees"), **comfort with ambiguity** ("we don't know how our business will evolve, and navigating Google internally requires dealing with a lot of ambiguity"), and **evidence that you've taken some courageous or interesting paths in your life."**

Breaking each down:

**1. Enjoying Fun** — Google employees work hard but are also playful. The culture celebrates levity, and candidates are expected to bring genuine warmth and energy to their interactions.

**2. Intellectual Humility** — This means having the curiosity and humility to know there is always something new to learn, and being open to accepting help, letting others lead, and trusting colleagues to do their jobs.

**3. Conscientiousness** — Google wants people who think and behave like *owners* — contributing beyond their role, holding themselves accountable, and getting things done without ego.

**4. Comfort with Ambiguity** — This means not just tolerating the unfamiliar but truly appreciating it — finding joy in being surprised and dealing with unforeseen circumstances rather than resisting them.

**5. Courageous or Interesting Paths** — Googleyness principles encourage people to be their authentic self and innovative. Google is not looking for people that fit a certain business mold — they want diversity of thought and experience.

#### Not "Culture Fit" — A Critical Distinction

Bock explicitly stressed that Googleyness was never about looking for people who "look like us." *"It isn't about whether you're a bro-grammer, or are just like us, or fit a narrow mold,"* he said. Rather, the goal is to discover people who are different, innovative, and who can challenge the status quo.

A one-sentence summary: *"Someone trustworthy who values and respects the differences in people, is curious and eager to learn, finds joy and the positive in day-to-day life, is innovative and comfortable with uncertainty."*

### How It Shows Up in Practice

Google found four distinct attributes that predicted whether someone would be successful on the job: general cognitive ability, leadership, Googleyness, and role-related knowledge. Once identified, all interview feedback was required to comment specifically on each one.

Googleyness is evaluated not just in formal interview rounds but in every interaction — how a candidate treats the receptionist, cafeteria staff, people passing in the hall. It's assessed through how you listen, communicate, and show genuine curiosity about the company and the people in it.

---

In short, Google's mission is an ambitious, utilitarian ideal — making all of human knowledge accessible to everyone. "Googleyness" is the human operating system they built to pursue it: curious, humble, fun, principled, and comfortable with not knowing all the answers.
